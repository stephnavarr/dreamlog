The Kitchen Table

Everything important in our family happened at that table. Most of it we never talked about.

There was a table in my mother’s kitchen that knew things. Not the kind of things you could put into sentences — more the kind that sit in the grain of wood, absorbed over thirty years of dinners and arguments and Tuesday mornings.

It was a round table, which I think was intentional on her part. Nobody at the head. Nobody left out. You could always pull up another chair.

We ate there every night until I was twelve, and then less, the way things become less when life accelerates into after-school jobs and friends who have cars. But I still remember the texture of those meals. The particular silence of my father when he was tired. The way my sister would wait until everyone was seated to say the one thing she’d been holding all day.

The table is gone now. My mother moved, and the new kitchen was smaller, and some things don’t survive transitions. But I find myself measuring rooms by it. Wondering if the table I have now — the one I eat alone at mostly, or with a friend when I remember to cook — is doing its job. Whether it’s absorbing the right things. Whether something, years from now, will carry the memory of these particular silences.

My grandmother had a table too. Formica, yellow. Practical in that postwar way. I only sat at it a handful of times, but I can still feel the cold of it in the mornings, the way she’d put your coffee down without asking if you wanted it.

I think about what gets passed down and how. Not just the tables themselves, but the idea that a table is where you put your weight. Where you arrive and stay a while. Where you learn what a family sounds like from the inside.
